PORTUGAL TRAVEL HUB · MANIFESTO

There are two Portugals.

The one that shows up on the booking sites — and the one behind it, waiting for whoever knows how to ask.

When you search for a hotel in Lisbon, why do you always see the same ten at the top of Booking?

It's not because they have better reviews. It's not because they're closer to the centre. It's not because they have better prices. And it's not because they're the best hotels in Lisbon — because, in most cases, they're not.

They appear there because they paid more to be there.

This isn't a conspiracy theory. It's how the model works, written in the contract every hotel signs. I know because I've been in hospitality for nearly 20 years. I've paid those commissions. I've seen the invoice at the end of the month. I've clicked the button that decides whether the property shows up on top or on page five.

What it actually costs to appear at the top

The model of the major booking sites is simple and perverse. The hotel pays a commission on every reservation — between 15% and 25% depending on volume and country. Then, if you want to appear at the top of the list, you pay an additional surcharge. In some European markets, the effective commission exceeds 25% per night.

Do the maths with one hundred euros. When you book a night for one hundred euros, the hotel might receive seventy-five — or less. The other twenty-five go to the platform that showed you the hotel.

And here's the part nobody tells you: that money doesn't disappear from your side. It comes from somewhere. It comes from breakfast. It comes from the room. It comes from the person who cleans, from maintenance time, from the towels that aren't replaced. The hotel paying 25% commission cannot give you the same experience it would give without it.

That's why so many stays in Portugal are fine — without ever being good.

Who wins when the traveller loses

The system rewards the hotel that pays most, not the hotel that delivers most. And it consistently rewards the same: international chains with margin to absorb high commissions, properties that sacrifice service to sustain the auction.

Small hotels — the country house in Alentejo, the boutique in Porto, the family-run guesthouse in Tavira — can't compete in that auction. And they don't appear. Not because they're worse, but because they don't pay to win.

It's a race to the bottom, and the traveller pays the bill without knowing it.

What Portugal Travel Hub does differently

Portugal Travel Hub has no auction. Partners pay a fixed monthly fee to be verified, not to appear higher. There is no boost. No sponsored. No "Preferred Partner".

Verification is simple and demanding: someone from the team visits the property, talks to recent guests, confirms that what's on the site is what's on the ground. If it isn't, the partner doesn't get in. If they get in and stop delivering, they leave.

It's the opposite of the dominant model. And that's why what you see on Portugal Travel Hub isn't what paid most to be there — it's what earned its place.
